Abstract: The paper analyzes the snail assemblages of 36 plant community types in the Biikk Mts, Hungary. The abiotic and biotic factors influencing spatial distribution along vegetational succession, and structural changes of assemblages during reforestation are examined. Different anthropogenic effects are detected. Differences between species composition of the various beechwoods of Europe are evaluated based on new and literature data. Introduction The analysis of the relationship between ter- restrial snail assemblages and plant communities is the primary objective of the present paper. The fauna of climatically zonal beechwoods of the Biikk Mts will be compared with snail as- semblages of European beechwoods ( Fagetum medio-europaeum ). The effect of hydrological and orographic factors upon species and species as- semblages is in the focus of this study. Structural changes of snail assemblages along plant succes- sion will also receive special attention. In addition to this analysis, proposals will be made for ap- propriate management of protected areas. Material and methods Field studies The study area includes all herbaceous and woody communities of the mountains from the pediment to the peaks, regardless their semi-natural or deteriorated state, forest management and land use type (forested, grazed, cut, or exposed to tourists). The number of plant community types examined is 36. Sampling for snails was performed by ten 25 x 25 cm quadrats at each site. Phytosociological status, exposition, slope angle, soil temperature on the surface, and closure of canopy and understorey were recorded. Addition- al data to be used are elevation above sea level, and water production data on springs and streams. Soil samples were also taken. The data basis consists of samples taken at 185 localities, representing 14 grassland, 9 edaphic bush- and gorge-forest, and 12 forest com- munities. The plant communities examined may be assigned into three successional seres: 1. sere along streams, 2. limestone sere, and 3. succes- sional sere developed on acidic rocks (diabase, gabbro, andesite), each from grasslands to com- pletely closed forests, including secular, zonal and intrazonal-edaphic communities. See Körmöczi (1992, this volume) for more information. Table 1 follows the numbering used by Körmöczi. Since Cotinus coggygria became dominant in com- munities 13 and 25, they were treated separately from the other Corno-Quercetum communities. Soil traps were used at several localities for 1-2 years, and were emptied every month. The forest types concerned are: Tilio-Fraxinetum (Csunya valley), Phyllitidi- Aceretum (Leány valley), Quer- cetum p. cerris (Sikfõkút), Melitti-Fagetum (Rej- tek, in forest and clearing as well). Traps were also used in three dolines of Nagymezõ and at the streamside in Szalajka valley. Changes in abundance (A/m2), average species number per quadrat ("species density"), Shannon - Wiener diversity and species number along the vegetation succession were monitored. The ecological W (humidity) and R (soil reaction) in- dicator values developed by Zólyomi et al. (1964) for vegetation types were also used. For studies between 1987-1990 the W values were calculated</page><page sequence="2">110 from averages of relative soil humidity, rather than from the indicator scores. The phyto- sociological nomenclature follows Soó (1964, 1980). The zoogeogaphic area analysis (Bába 1982, 1989) revealed the role of fauna area types in the dif- ferent vegetation types. Statistical analysis Classification and ordination methods were ap- plied to evaluate the data. The ecological and area geographic observations and conclusions were supplemented with cluster analysis. The complete linkage method and information theory clustering (Podani 1980) were used based on presence/ab- sence and abundance data to evaluate the relationships among mollusc assemblages of dif- ferent vegetation types. For Zoogeographie analysis the Czekanowski index (in average linkage) and the Renkonen index (simple average) were adopted (see also Bába 1989). Based on the classification of species and sites, a rearranged data matrix was prepared and evaluated for sharpness of block structure by analysis of concentration (Feoli &amp; Orlóci 1979, program REBLOCK, Podani 1991). The chi- square test was used to explain site groupings by species groups. Since rare species were clustered together even though they never co-occurred in any site, some corrections were performed on the table (18% of species). From the table the follow- ing ecological species groups were identifiable: 1. hygrophilous shade species, 2. mesohygrophilous species of the beechwood zone, 3. xeromeso- philous species of open areas, 4. ubiquist species (Bába &amp; Podani 1986, and see Table 1). Principal components analysis was used as an ordination technique (program PRINCOMP, Podani 1991) to evaluate relationships between species and hydrological and orographic factors (Bába &amp; Podani 1986). Principal coordinates analysis was also applied to the snaill assemblage data from limestone and acidic substrates (program PRIN- COOR, Podani 1991). Area-geographic evaluation The constancy of species was computed based on the presence of snail species in different vegeta- tion types. For each locality, the dominance of species was also calculated. For describing struc- tural changes three constancy groups were distin- guished: 1) accidental species (below 10%); 2) Bába: Terrestrial snail assemblages subconstant species (10-50%), 3) constant species (over 50%). Constant-dominant species are those that exceed 50% constancy and 10% dominance. Differential species were selected from species of 25-50% constancy and high dominance. Although considered subconstant-subdominant, these species are important in distinguishing among snail assemblages. The analysis of area types followed Bába (1982, 1989). The evaluation of succession used Lozek's (1964) ecological species groups, amalgamated into three major groups: species of open areas, grasslands and rocky slopes (0, xSf), species of shrubby com- munities (S, Sw, OW, W(s), M, Wf, Bw), and the species occurring only in forests (W, W(h), W(M), H, P). These groups are useful in examining the forestation processes along the successional seres (Table 1). Snail assemblages in European beechwoods Using literature data on the snail fauna of European beechwoods, this paper will provide a general review. Although the different reports are based on different sampling designs, they seem comparable in terms of the presence and absence of species. All publications were used that reported the exact beechwood forest type as well. The review uses data from 223 forest stands from 36 mountains of Europe. Species and their distributions Species Field studies in the Bükk Mts yielded a collection of 31,570 individuals belonging to 93 species. The living specimens account for 65.3%. The soil traps contained 1725 specimens. The ten years of research resulted in the detection of some species new to the mountains. Permanent humidity, less disturbance and limited forest use play a role in this. Also, the grasslands are on steep slopes. Of the zonal vegetation types only undisturbed old stands have rare species (e.g., Melitti-Fagetum in Csunya valley, Ispán hill, and Leány valley). Factors influencing geographic distribution In addition to the cool climate of northern slopes and gorges, the depth of soils also influences the species composition. This is why the grasslands and bush forests, which have shallow soils, are separated from the others in the dendrogram. The hygrophilous species are concentrated along streams and springs. The effect of altitude and water production of springs and streams upon snail assemblages was evaluated by principal components analysis. Species positively correlated with water produc- tion are 9, 37, 38, 42, 54, 56, 58, 65, and 76 (species belonging to the hygrophilous and mesohygrophilous eco-groups). There are 21 species positively correlated with altitude, the ex- ceptions are species 3, 5, 17 and 20 (Table 1) that are ubiquists of rock grasslands and forests of loose canopy. Species 6, 10, 12, 22, 25, 26, 67, 69, 73, 74, 76, 78, 80-82, and 88 are mesohygrophilous species of the beechwood-zone which belong to the Pontic-Mediterranean and European mon- tane Zoogeographie groups. They can descend only on the northern slopes and in gorges together with the intrazonal vegetation. Zoogeographie analysis of species distribution Based on collections before 1970 and recorded in a grid, Kövecses &amp; Varga (1984) detected by a chi- square test that the fauna of the Bükk Mts is fairly homogeneous. As altitude increases, because oceanic air masses dominate the climate in two seasons (cf. Péczely</page><page sequence="7">Abstracta Botanica 16 (1992) 1981), the submediterranean fauna groups from montane refugia (Ponto-Mediterranean, Holo- mediterranean, Central European montane) in- crease in percentage. The change of sub- mediterranean fauna elements, which belong to the hygrophilous and hydromesophilous ecogroups, is especially significant. Based on our knowledge on the appearance of Lozek's fauna elements in the Holocene, the interpretability of area analytical results (Bába &amp; Füköh 1984), and the pollen analysis of plant communities in the Holocene (Zólyomi 1952, 1958), we can conclude that 68% of the Atlantic dominant species are from the montane Fagiott iltyricum, Adria to- Mediterranean and European montane fauna groups. These belong to the hygrophilous, meso- hygrophilous and riparian hygrophilous ecogroups. The fauna of slopes, especially that of the southern and southwestern slopes, is influenced by short illumination time and low irradiation (Justyák &amp; Tar 1974), increasing the possibility for establishing submediterranean elements. In oakwoods and grasslands ( Quercetum petraeae- cerris, Qu.-Carpinetum) of hilltops, the proportion of continental elements increases, because temperature fluctuations exceed fluctuations on slopes (Bába 1989). 115 Similarities among snail assemblages The snail assemblages of vegetation types were analyzed from several aspects in order to facilitate recognition of successional trends. Bush forests, grasslands, riparian communities, zonal and intrazonal forests (105 sites, 19 com- munities) were analyzed by clustering based on presence/absence and density, resulting in two main groups (Fig. 1). Abundance , diversity, species number and density. These characteristics appear to be correlated (Figures 6- 7), with trends differing with the suc- cessional seres. Increase or decrease of humidity and anthropogenic effects increase the paired characteristics in the assemblages of the zonal sere (15, 16, 17; Fig. 6). This is not so in the in- trazonal communities. In the riparian sere, paral- lel with drying out, abundance and species density decrease but species number and diversity exhibit an increasing trend, except in the secondary com- munities (Fig. 6). On limy and acidic substrates, the reasons for oscillating curves are anthro- pogenic effects (trampling by tourists, litter, syl- vicultural activity, especially on acidic rocks), as well as the existence of stationary communities and the retreat of rock grasslands and bush forests. On limestone, the high density value of Fes- tucetum glaucae (7) at Tarkõ is caused by acciden- tal elements immigrated from the neighboring Tilio-Fraxinetum. There are many disappearing species in the Melitti- Fagetum and Aconito-Fagetum submontane and montane beechwoods, and in intrazonal stands of Filipendulo-Geranietum and Seslerio- Fagetum developed thanks to edaphic and local humidity conditions. Possibilities of nature protection To diminish anthropogenic influence strongly dis- turbing the snail assemblages, I suggest the fol- lowing. Protection of riparian communities along roads (the Petasitetum and Alnetum communities are endangered mostly). Reduction of cutting and thinning is strongly recommended especially in protected areas. The snail fauna is the richest in riparian communities, because of the fauna transport via rivers (Bába et al. 1982-1983, Bába 1979). Reforested areas should be connected to the planted or intact forest stands along rivers, to allow resettlement of snail species. Tourism must be reduced near stationary grasslands (Festucetum vaginatae). Mowing should leave at least 5-8 cm high grass on Nagymezõ to prevent the appearance of shrubs, and to maintain the habitat for shade species. In- trazonal communities are more affected by little Pertubation than zonal forests growing on deep soils. These may function as protecting forests only.
